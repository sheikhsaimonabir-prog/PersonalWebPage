Energy Management Systems Review

Overview

This project provides a comprehensive study of Building Energy Management Systems (BEMS) with the aim of understanding how modern buildings can achieve efficient, intelligent, and sustainable energy use. The work begins by examining the global context of rising energy demand and environmental concerns, establishing the need for advanced energy management solutions in both residential and commercial buildings. It then explores the fundamental architecture of BEMS, including data collection through sensors, IoT-based communication, and centralized platforms for monitoring and control. A significant portion of the project is dedicated to reviewing existing literature, international standards such as ISO 50001, and regulatory frameworks that guide the implementation of energy management systems. Core system functionalities such as real-time monitoring, automated control of HVAC and lighting systems, energy forecasting, and fault detection are analyzed to understand their role in improving energy efficiency. The study also highlights how these systems contribute to measurable energy savings, typically in the range of 10–20%, while maintaining occupant comfort. In addition, the project evaluates several commercial and open-source EMS solutions, comparing their features, usability, and practical applications. These systems are assessed based on their ability to provide data visualization, predictive analytics, and integration with smart devices and renewable energy sources. Through this analysis, the project identifies key trends and technological advancements shaping the future of smart buildings. Overall, this work offers a structured and practical understanding of energy management systems, demonstrating how data-driven and automated approaches can significantly enhance building performance, reduce operational costs, and support global sustainability goals.

Methodology

The methodology of this project is based on a structured review and comparative analysis approach. The study begins with an extensive literature review of existing research papers to understand the current state of Building Energy Management Systems (BEMS), their design strategies, and reported performance in terms of energy savings. This is followed by an examination of relevant international standards and regulations, particularly ISO 50001 and European building energy performance directives, to establish the regulatory and operational framework within which EMS solutions are developed and implemented. Subsequently, the project analyzes the core architecture and functionalities of energy management systems, including data collection through sensors, IoT-based communication, data processing, and control mechanisms. Different functional layers such as monitoring, control, analysis, and optimization are systematically studied to identify their roles and contributions to overall system performance. A comparative evaluation of existing commercial and open-source EMS platforms is then conducted. Various software and service providers are reviewed based on their features, system capabilities, user interfaces, and practical applications in real-world scenarios. This includes assessing tools for data visualization, forecasting, automation, and integration with smart devices. Finally, insights from literature, standards, and system comparisons are synthesized to identify key trends, effective strategies, and limitations of current energy management systems. This integrated approach enables a comprehensive understanding of how BEMS operate and how they can be utilized to improve energy efficiency and sustainability in buildings.

Tools Used

The analysis in this project is based on a combination of literature review tools, data interpretation approaches, and software-based evaluation of existing Energy Management Systems (EMS). Academic databases and published research papers are used as primary sources to extract information on system performance, methodologies, and energy savings. In addition, standards such as ISO 50001 and regulatory documents are analyzed to establish evaluation criteria. The study relies on comparative analysis techniques rather than experimental simulations, focusing on interpreting reported results, system architectures, and functional capabilities. The tools evaluated in this project consist mainly of commercial and open-source EMS platforms and software solutions used for building energy monitoring and control. These include Home Assistant, Strømkontroll (Futurehome), openMaint, BEE2Energy, Homey, RETScreen, and Energy Lens. Each of these tools is assessed based on key functionalities such as data collection from sensors and IoT devices, real-time monitoring, data visualization, automation and control of building systems (e.g., HVAC and lighting), forecasting capabilities, and reporting features. The evaluation focuses on how effectively these tools support energy efficiency, user interaction, and decision-making in practical building environments.

Results

(1) Building Energy Management Systems (BEMS) enable continuous monitoring and intelligent control, leading to typical energy savings of approximately 10–20% in buildings

(2) The most effective features contributing to energy reduction include scheduled and occupancy-based control, HVAC optimization, and energy price–based operation strategies

(3) Advanced approaches such as forecasting, model predictive control, and fault detection further enhance system performance and efficiency

(4) Evaluated tools demonstrate strong capabilities in real-time monitoring, data visualization, automation, and integration with IoT devices and renewable energy sources

(5) Overall performance depends on system sophistication, with advanced EMS platforms delivering greater efficiency, cost savings, and sustainability benefits
